Investigative Outcomes
Complaint investigation data from the Philadelphia Police Department classifies investigations into three different groups: Sustained Finding, No Sustained Findings, and Investigation Pending.
Disciplinary Outcomes
Among cases where the department's investigation yields a Sustained Finding, the complainant(s) must appear as a witness at a Police Board of Inquiry hearing. which makes a guilty/not guilty recommendation. The report is then sent to a Commanding Officer, responsible for deciding on disciplinary action. The department data classifies these disciplinary outcomes into four groups: Guilty Finding, Training/Counseling, No Guilty Findings, and Discipline Pending
Guilty Findings
Guilty Findings include all sustained investigations resulting in an officer being reprimanded, terminated, suspended or facing criminal charges. The data provided by PPD makes no distinction between these outcomes, so there is no way to determine how many cases result in termination or criminal charges, except that it is less than or equal to the number of Guilty Findings. These cases account for just 2% of investigation outcomes.

That rate also changes significantly depending on who is making the complaint, even accounting for the type of complaint made.
White Complainants
Complaints filed by White complainants result in a guilty finding in 3.2% of investigations.
Black Complainants
Meanwhile, for Black complainants, just 1.2% of investigations result in a guilty finding (40% the rate of their White counterparts).
Black Complainant/White Officer
This number shrinks even further for complaints filed specifically against White officers.

Just 0.6% of investigations against White officers filed by Black complainants result in a guilty finding.
White Complainant/Black Officer
Conversely, investigations against Black officers filed by White complainants are six-and-a-half times more likely to result in a guilty finding than those filed against White officers by Black complainants.

These investigations are also 45% more likely to result in a sustained finding.
The Full Picture
This visual represents each disciplinary investigation as a single tile.

They can be filtered by date, using the timeline at the top or by type of investigation, using the options on the top-right.
Stories Behind The Complaints
Hovering over any tile will display full information about that complaint, including a summary depicting the events leading up to the complaint.

It's important to recognize that these stories are based only on typed notes by a member of the department and that as of 2019, Philadelphia began redacting full details of complaints and replacing them with boilerplate 1-2 sentence summaries. Full summaries for complaints before July 2018 are provided, courtesy of Philly Declaration's Police Accountability Project, which published datasets before the city began redacting summaries.
Organizing The Data
You can sort these tiles by a number of features and observe some of the same pattern explored above.

Here, the chart has been sorted by complainant race. Recall that a complainant being black was the second strongest indicator of whether an investigation would result in a guilty finding (trailing only whether the officer was white).

Hover over the section headers for summary information. Use the dropdown on the legend box (top-left) to change the feature the tiles are sorted on.
Overdue Investigations
According to Philadelphia Law, investigations must be completed by Internal Affairs within seventy-five days of the complaint filing date.

The tiles have been filtered to include only investigations with complaint dates over two years old. Despite that, there are over three dozen investigations that are still pending (or were still pending as of the city's last public update).
Complaint Classifications
Here the complaints have been filtered to include only those classified as "Physical Abuse", "Verbal Abuse", "Criminal Allegation", "Sexual Crime/Misconduct", and "Civil Rights Complaint". There is a small, but statistically significant difference in both the rate of Sustained Finding and Guilty Finding between these complaints and others.

These complaints are handled by the department's Internal Affairs division, which conducts an investigation before determining whether or not an allegation is sustained. Sustained allegations are then given to the Police Board of Inquiry (PBI), which makes a disciplinary recommendation after an informal hearing.

The city began publishing data about these complaints online in 2018, including redacted and anonymized data for all complaints since 2013. Over the course of more than seven years represented in the published data, just 16% of investigations have resulted in a sustained finding and just 2% have resulted in a guilty finding by the PBI.

Though limited, the data provided by the department does contain some contextual information about complaints. An analysis performed on this data, using a Random Forest classifier, found that the single most important feature in determining if an investigation would result in a Guilty Finding was whether or not the accused officer was White. The next most important feature was whether or not the complainant was Black.
